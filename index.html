Coronary Artery Disease

Make sure to check with your cardiologist!!!

With today's knowledge and resources, you can take powerful steps to protect your heart. Small, consistent changes in how you eat, move, and manage stress can dramatically alter your health trajectory.

Take care of your coronary artery disease by:
  • Taking all prescribed medications regularly and attending follow-up appointments with your cardiologist.
  • Managing stress through techniques like meditation, deep breathing exercises, or counseling services.
  • Quitting smoking and avoiding secondhand smoke to significantly reduce your risk of further heart damage.
  • Exercising regularly with at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week, as approved by your physician.
  • Eating a heart-healthy diet low in saturated fats, sodium, and cholesterol. Focus on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.

About Coronary Artery Disease

The Silent Threat: Asymptomatic CAD

Many people with CAD experience no symptoms until the disease is advanced. This "silent CAD" makes early detection through risk factor assessment crucial, especially if you have family history. Regular check-ups can catch problems before symptoms appear.

What is Coronary Artery Disease?

A disease wherein the heart has insufficient oxygen delivery due to damaged or clogged arteries. Unhealthy arteries are results of high blood pressure, high cholesterol, obesity or genes (CAD present in family history). Consequently, the arteries get clogged, narrowed, or tightened in ways that disable or lessen oxygen delivery.

Lifestyle Factors That Lead to CAD:

  • Unhealthy diet: High cholesterol foods
  • Smoking or exposure to second-hand smoke
  • Sedentary lifestyle: Less physical activity
  • Stress or lack of sleep: Tightens vessels & raises blood pressure
  • Excess intake of alcohol or tobacco: Releases triglycerides in the blood and develops fatty deposits or plaques on artery walls

Preventing the Risk of CAD

  • Avoid smoking, drinking, tobacco
  • Exercise to regulate blood pressure levels
  • Avoid stress & sleep on time
  • Eat a balanced diet
  • Avoid refined sugars & excessive intake of added sugars

Treatments for CAD

Medication:

  • Blood thinning medicines: Aspirin & clopidogrel decrease blood clotting so blood flows smoothly
  • Statins: To lower cholesterol levels
  • Vasodilators (Nitrates): Widen arteries to increase pressure

Interventional Procedures/Surgeries:

  • Coronary Angioplasty: A deflated balloon is inserted in the artery & inflated to push fatty deposits outward, widening the artery. A metal stent (wire mesh tube) secures the artery to avoid collapse.
  • Coronary artery bypass: Blood is diverted from the damaged artery to a healthy blood vessel (usually from the leg - saphenous vein - or chest - internal mammary artery)
  • Heart transplant: A severely damaged heart is replaced with a donor heart

Is CAD Genetic or Hereditary?

Conditions that lead to it include Type 1 diabetes and hypertension which are genetic and can eventually develop into CAD. However, if an individual counters the effect of their genetic diseases by exercising and eating healthy, they can completely prevent CAD.

The DNA passed by our parents is unchangeable. However, as we age, our DNA sequences slightly differ from the ones we were born with. In short, the risk changes with lifestyle but is considered hereditary.

NIH Fact: "60-75% of offspring develop heart diseases due to family history."
